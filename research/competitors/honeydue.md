# Honeydue — Spending Tracker Profile

**Website:** https://www.honeydue.com
**Pricing:** Free — no paid tier, no paywalled features
**Platforms:** iOS, Android (no web app)
**Data connectivity:** Plaid (primary); claims 20,000+ supported institutions across 6 countries
**Last updated:** February 2026

---

## Positioning & Core Value Prop

Honeydue markets itself as "the simplest way for couples to manage money, together." It is purpose-built for two-person households — romantic partners or any two people with shared financial goals — who want a single view of their combined finances without fully merging them. The headline promise is transparency and communication: link your accounts, choose exactly what your partner sees, track bills together, and chat about transactions inside the app. It is not a budgeting-first tool in the YNAB mold; it leads with visibility and conversation rather than envelope allocation.

---

## Key Features

- Linked account dashboard showing both partners' bank accounts, credit cards, loans, and investments in one feed, with per-account privacy controls
- Per-account sharing settings: each account can be set to share balances and transactions, share balance only, or hidden entirely from the partner
- Monthly category spending limits (not zero-based budgets — simple soft caps per category) with push notifications as limits are approached
- Bill tracking: recurring bills entered manually with due dates; both partners receive reminders before payments are due
- In-app chat between partners about finances, plus per-transaction comments and emoji reactions
- Automatic transaction import and rule-based categorization across 17 preset categories (editable names, custom categories allowed)
- Color-coded category donut chart in the Activity tab for a quick visual spending breakdown by category and by month
- Joint Cash account (formerly Honeydue Joint Banking): a shared FDIC-insured account with individual Visa debit cards per partner, issued via Sutton Bank — though the debit card program was discontinued in August 2023 and is no longer available to new users
- Supports cash transaction manual entry

---

## Spending Visibility & Look-Back Experience

Honeydue provides a basic but functional backward-looking transaction view. The Activity tab shows a chronological transaction feed across all linked accounts, with category breakdowns and a monthly summary chart. Users can filter by month and by account. There is no dedicated analytical look-back experience: no trend view, no month-over-month comparison charts, no year-to-date summaries, and no way to ask "what did we spend on groceries over the last three months." The reporting is limited to the current month's category donut chart and a simple transaction list. Historical data retention is a known limitation — users report the app retains only around 6 months of data, with no way to access longer-term history. There is also no data export capability, which blocks any workaround through spreadsheets or external tools. The app does not require upfront budget setup to show you data (transactions appear as accounts are linked), but the look-back depth is shallow — you get immediate visibility of recent spending but almost nothing beyond a rolling few months.

## Household / Multi-User Support

This is Honeydue's core differentiator — and it is strictly a two-person model.

**How it works:** Both partners create separate accounts and are linked via an email invitation. Once linked, each partner sees a unified dashboard that pulls from both users' connected accounts. Crucially, accounts are not merged — they remain individually owned and labeled. Each user manages their own bank connections and independently controls the visibility of each account they own.

**Privacy controls:** Per-account privacy is granular. For each account you add, you choose one of three visibility levels: share balance and transactions with partner, share balance only, or hide entirely. Individual transactions can also be hidden. This lets couples maintain financial privacy on personal accounts while sharing visibility on joint or household spending accounts.

**Whose account is whose:** The app labels accounts by owner, so both partners always know which transactions belong to which person. There is no anonymous pooling — provenance is preserved.

**Communication layer:** Honeydue includes an in-app messaging thread for general financial conversation, plus per-transaction commenting. Partners can flag a transaction with an emoji or leave a question directly on the transaction — a lightweight accountability and communication feature that no other personal finance app offers natively.

**Hard ceiling — two users only:** Honeydue cannot be expanded to three or more users. There is no concept of children, teenagers, or other household members. It cannot serve a family of three or four, only a pair. Some users have applied it to non-romantic pairings (parent/adult child, roommates, caregiver/elder), but the model is architecturally limited to exactly two participants. This is the most significant structural limitation relative to family-scale use cases.

## AI & Intelligence Features

**Transaction categorization**

Rule-based with no meaningful learning capability. Transactions are auto-categorized into one of 17 preset buckets, but the system is widely criticized for poor accuracy and a critical bug: corrections do not persist. Multiple users report manually recategorizing the same merchant (e.g., Whole Foods) repeatedly, only to have it revert to the wrong category on the next sync. The app does not learn from user corrections. Merchant name enrichment (clean display names, logos) appears minimal. This is among the most-cited frustrations in App Store and Trustpilot reviews.

**Natural language interface**

None. There is no chat assistant, no query interface, and no way to ask the app a question about your spending in plain language. The only conversational feature is the partner-to-partner chat, which is a human-to-human communication channel with no AI involvement.

**AI-generated insights**

None. The app does not proactively surface spending patterns, anomalies, or behavioral observations. There are no AI-written summaries, no alerts based on detected changes in behavior, and no month-over-month narrative commentary. Reporting is limited to the static category donut chart and monthly totals.

**Perks / transaction intelligence**

None. Honeydue has no awareness of credit card benefits, statement credits, or card-specific perks. There is no detection of whether a credit card's annual travel credit has been used, no tracking of subscription benefits, and no connection between transaction data and card reward structures.

**Overall AI maturity**

AI is entirely absent from Honeydue — the product is a rule-based transaction aggregator with a couples communication layer and no machine learning or generative AI capabilities of any kind.

---

## Card Perks / Credits Tracking

Not present. Honeydue has no credit card perks or benefits tracking whatsoever. The app does not know what cards a user holds, what benefits those cards offer, whether annual credits have been used, or whether a given transaction is relevant to a card benefit. This feature category is a complete gap.

---

## Account & Bank Connectivity

Honeydue uses Plaid as its primary data aggregator and claims support for 20,000+ financial institutions across 6 countries. In practice, connectivity reliability is a major and recurring complaint. Users report:

- Bank accounts failing to sync for days at a time with no notification
- Balances displayed inaccurately (one user reported their balance shown as 25% higher than actual)
- Accounts frequently disconnecting and requiring re-authentication
- No real-time balance updates — syncs happen on a delay
- Specific issues with Chase, some credit unions, and institutions that require MFA

When connectivity breaks, users report support directing them to reconnect manually with no root cause explanation. The support team's responsiveness has reportedly deteriorated significantly since 2023, with some users receiving no reply to support emails for weeks. The debit card and joint banking product (Honeydue Joint Banking, issued by Sutton Bank) was discontinued in August 2023, removing one of the app's more differentiated banking-layer features.

## Business Model & Pricing

Honeydue is fully free with no paid tier, no premium upgrade path, and no features behind a paywall. There are no ads displayed in the app. The company was acquired by Mission Lane (a credit card and digital banking fintech) in May 2021 for an undisclosed sum. Mission Lane's stated rationale was leveraging Honeydue's digital banking and account aggregation capabilities to build out its own debit card product (Mission Money). The acquisition appears to have deprioritized Honeydue as a standalone product: the joint debit card feature was discontinued in 2023, the in-app support chat was removed, and the product has received minimal new feature development since. Revenue model for the standalone Honeydue app is unclear; it likely functions as an acquisition or retention vehicle for Mission Lane rather than as a standalone revenue-generating product. The company was founded in 2016, went through Y Combinator's S17 batch, raised approximately $120K in seed funding, and had approximately 500,000 registered users at the time of acquisition.

## User Sentiment

**Loves:**
- Genuinely free with no paywalls or feature limitations — repeatedly praised as "so much better than YNAB and free"
- The couples-specific shared visibility model is unique and fills a real gap; no other major app handles the two-person household as a first-class concept
- Per-transaction comments and emoji reactions with a partner are a small but beloved feature that makes money conversations feel lower-stakes
- Bill reminders with shared visibility help couples avoid missed payments
- Setup is fast — accounts link quickly and the shared view is immediately legible
- App Store rating: 4.5/5 on iOS (based on a substantial review volume)

**Complaints:**
- Auto-categorization is inaccurate and corrections do not persist — identified as the top functional frustration
- Bank sync is unreliable; accounts disconnect frequently, balances lag or display incorrectly
- No web app — mobile-only is a hard limitation for users who want to review finances on a computer
- Only 6 months of historical data retained; no trend views, no year-over-year analysis
- No data export (CSV or otherwise) — no escape hatch to external tools
- No month-over-month or longitudinal reporting; the single-month donut chart is the extent of analytics
- Customer support has gone effectively dark since 2023; multiple users report weeks without a response
- The joint debit card feature (a differentiator) was discontinued with little notice in August 2023
- Hard-limited to two users — cannot add children, other family members, or additional adults
- Google Play rating: 3.6/5, significantly lower than iOS
- Some users flagging that the app shows signs of pre-shutdown deterioration similar to Honeyfi/Firstly before their consumer app closure

## Strengths (Relative to What We're Building)

- **The shared-visibility model is the right instinct.** Honeydue correctly identifies that couples need to see each other's accounts in one place with labeled ownership, not merge everything into an anonymous pool. The per-account privacy toggle (share everything / balance only / hidden) is a genuinely smart design that we should study and likely replicate or exceed.
- **Per-transaction communication is unique and valuable.** The ability to comment on or react to a specific transaction between partners is a feature no other major personal finance app ships. It solves a real pain point: the awkward text message about a confusing charge. This is table-stakes for any household-aware product.
- **Free with no friction drives adoption.** The zero-cost model removes the biggest barrier to getting a second person onboarded. If we require both household members to subscribe, we create a conversion problem Honeydue sidesteps entirely.
- **Bill tracking with shared reminders** is a practical, underrated feature — recurring bills are a shared household concern that most budgeting apps treat as a single-user problem.

---

## Gaps (Relative to What We're Building)

- **Hard two-user ceiling.** Honeydue cannot be used by families with children, blended households, or any group beyond a pair. Our household-aware model must support n users with role-appropriate visibility — this is a fundamental architectural difference.
- **No look-back depth.** With only ~6 months of history, no trend views, no year-over-year comparisons, and no data export, Honeydue cannot support the "understand your past spending" use case that is central to our product. We must offer multi-year history with rich analytical surface area.
- **No AI whatsoever.** Categorization is rule-based and broken. There is no NLP querying, no generative insights, no anomaly detection. Our AI-native approach — conversational querying, smart categorization that learns, proactive pattern surfacing — represents a fundamentally different product tier.
- **No card perks tracking.** This is a complete blank for Honeydue. Our ability to auto-detect and track statement credits and card benefits is a pure differentiator with zero overlap.
- **Mobile-only.** No web app limits use for the planning and review sessions where couples often want a larger screen. A web experience should be part of our roadmap.
- **Stagnant development and poor support.** The product appears to be in quiet decline post-acquisition. Bank sync reliability, the removal of the joint debit card, and a non-responsive support team suggest Mission Lane is not prioritizing Honeydue as a growth product. Users actively seeking alternatives create an addressable audience.
- **No spending analytics beyond the current month.** A single monthly donut chart is the entirety of Honeydue's analytical output. We should offer look-back analytics by time period, merchant, category, and account owner at minimum.

---

## Overall Assessment

Honeydue is the most direct predecessor to what we are building — it correctly identified that couples need a shared financial view with granular privacy controls and in-app communication — but it is functionally thin, technologically dated, and appears to be in slow decline following its 2021 acquisition by Mission Lane. It has no AI capabilities, no look-back analytics depth, no card perks awareness, and an architectural ceiling of exactly two users that excludes families entirely. The most important takeaway is that Honeydue has validated the market demand for household-aware shared finance tools while simultaneously demonstrating how much room exists above it: any product that combines Honeydue's couples-sharing model with genuine AI intelligence, multi-year history, family-scale multi-user support, and card perks tracking would be categorically superior.
